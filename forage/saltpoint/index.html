Gypsy Camp
Mushroom Hunt Notes

with Justin Segall
Salt Point, California

A favorite mushroom hunting spot on the coast is Salt Point State Park. After celebrating Jasmin’s birthday evening with great food, campfire stories and spirits, we hit the sack in styled-out Sibly tents. In the morning, I lead a foray into the woods to see if we could contribute to the next meal with the best free food in the forest, mushrooms. Salt Point has a very diverse ecosystem that mushrooms love, and after a good rain you can find varieties like Chanterelles, Black Trumpets, Porcinis, Headhogs, Oyster’s, Candy Caps, Yellow Foots. Once out of pure luck I dug up a California White Truffle.

Before we left I warned everyone that the season has not yet begun, and not to get their hopes up because it has not rained for months. However, the moisture from the fog has made it ideal for warmer climate mushrooms, and it was a treat to find so many varieties of boletes. When I’m hunting for mushrooms I look for a good dense mix of foliage: Fir trees, oak trees, laurel and huckleberry bushes.

Most choice mushrooms are mycorrhizal. Mycorrhizal mushrooms work symbiotically with the rootlets of trees and plants, exchanging nutrients and extracting sugars. The forest relies on this exchange. These types of mushrooms include Chanterelles, Black Trumpets, Porcinis and Headhogs. Knowing that those varieties are mycorrhizal, you look around and under those types of trees and dig a little in the duff or leaf litter.

The other two types of mushrooms are parasitic (those that feed on living organisms, usually trees) and saprophytic. Saprophytic fungi are nature’s recyclers. They replenish the soil by breaking down complex organic matter into simpler reusable compounds. Mycologist Paul Stamets has been using saprophytic fungi to clean toxic sites and teaching its benefits of eco-restoration.

Mushrooms are the reproductive structures - or “fruit” – of certain fungi. Fungi do not manufacture their own food like plants. In this respect they are like animals: They must obtain food from outside sources. The part of the mushroom fungus that digests nutrients is an intricate web of fine threads collectively called mycelium. Mycelium may live for a few days to several hundred years if there is enough available moisture.

A few tips for mushroom hunting:
- go on a foray a few times with a mycology society
- learn a few varieties first that are very easy to identify
- know your trees
- also know how to identify poison oak.

The field guidebook we used on this trip that is also great for the Pacific Northwest is called All That the Rain Promises and More by David Arora.
