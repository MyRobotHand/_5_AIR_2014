An Interview with
Chris Christenson

Tiny Atlas Quarterly: Why do you choose to live and work where you do in North County?
Chris Christenson: I love living here in Cardiff. It has a mellow, small-town vibe, kinda sleepy, good food, and a variety of terrain for surfing. We have reef breaks, beach breaks, a river mouth and a point break all within a two and a half mile stretch that picks up most swell angles. It’s also only an hour and a half to LA, which is not too far, yet not too close.

Is your home your favorite surf spot or do you have places you always want to go back to, if time allows?
It’s always great to score good waves in my home town, but I've spent a lot of time on the road exploring new adventures for both surf and snow. Indonesia is, by far, my favorite. Fiji is probably a close second. I'm also trying to find good waves in unusual spots around the world. Last winter I explored a small island (can't name the spot) north of Japan just 60 miles from Russia and found some amazing palces. Only bummer was the water was just 37 degrees, but I still managed to stay out for two hours in a dry suit. The frostbite on my lip took two months to heal though, haha.

What part of the world do you like to travel to and, other than surfing, what do you like to do there? What are some of your favorite travels in the past few years?
I don’t limit my traveling to only surf destinations. I’m very into snowboarding in the back-country and try to do 2 or 3 international snow trips per year. I also have a house in the Sierras and live there part time throughout the year. I have a shaping room up there, so I can still get my work done. I try to schedule where I'll be around the swell and snow forecasts. The amazing thing about being a surfer and snowboarder/skier in California is that usually the swells come first, the snowstorm follows. We always get a huge snow dump shortly after an Eddie swell. Shhh, that’s a secret:) The last time they held the Eddie (a big wave tournament in memory of surfer Eddie Aikau), we obviously got a huge swell both in Hawaii and Cali and then a solid 8-foot dump in the Sierras! The Eddie in 2009 was an amazing week in December for me, not to mention that my team rider Greg Long won the competition! I was there and board caddied for him. Probably the best experience in my whole career.

When you are not shaping, what hobbies are driving your interests and do you find yourself bringing parts of those new hobbies back into your shaping thinking?
I'm also into fishing and motorcycles. The motorcycles are a good creative outlet for me, away from designing surfboards. The two go hand in hand, my inspirations and my California lifestyle. Currently I have a 53 Triumph Thunderbird, 69 Triumph Trophy, and a custom Harley chopper. Riding is great great escape for me: to let the wind blow the toxic surfboard fumes off of me and just let my mind escape from the day-to-day business grind. I try to build my lifestyle around the seasons. That being said, I love fishing off of San Diego and the Northern Baja coast from June ‘til late October. We actually have some world-class sport fishing here in San Diego, and I try to get out every week during the season. I caught limits of Yellowtail last Sunday.

You are partly known for shaping unusual boards and have mentioned admiring boards that are technically difficult to ride but are epic when you nail them (you have mentioned a parallel with pro golf clubs here). What designs are you interested in shaping recently and who are some riders you are working with these days?
I'm always trying to evolve as a shaper by keeping my designs fresh and avoiding a regressive or stagnant state. The paddle-in gun shapes have been a lot of fun to work on the last few years. Seeing my equipment and team replace the use of jet skis and tow ropes by paddling into waves at Jaws and Cortes Bank – considered impossible by many - has been very rewarding.  But honestly, I approach all of my designs with equal passion and avoid shapes that I'm not passionate about. I don't have any models that are strictly for filling a void in my business model for extra profits. I only design and shape boards that I am passionate about and enjoy riding myself. Some of my current riders are: Greg and Rusty Long, Ian Walsh, Brad Gerlach, Sterling Spencer, Mark Healey, Mitch Abshere, Daize Shayne Goodwin, Aamion Goodwin, Scotty Stopnik, and Mikey Detemple.

What materials and processes are you using now that you were not using a few years ago?
The materials for the most part are still the same ones I was using 20 years ago. There have been steady improvements with EPA standards, and lighter materials are now available, but I'm really hoping that we can find less toxic materials to work with that also build good surfboards. There are several "green" alternative materials out there to build boards with, but I have not found anything new that can perform at a pro level or that can build a stronger board. At the end of the day, a surfboard that lasts and works the best is the most environmentally friendly board. And more than our environment, I'm concerned about the health effects. Many of us board builders may be, or are being affected by the materials we have to use.  The next time you order a board from your local shaper, please consider the health risks he/she is taking before you ask for a discount.

The industry keeps changing with new technology, but you have said that the definition of “moving forward” is really subjective. What in shaping means moving forward to you right now?
Being a leader and not a follower. I'm really not concerned with what anyone else is building or riding. I never settle on a design. Once something is developed, I continue to try to make it better one shape at a time.

You have learned shaping from some masters. Have they imparted any lessons that still resonate with you?
Keeping it simple is what I was taught from the masters like Skip Frye and Dick Brewer early on, and I try to keep it that way in both business and design. I have 23 employees now, which definitely challenges me on "keeping it simple" but I have to give a lot of credit to my crew for getting my brand where it is today. They truly help us all keep it simple in the grand scheme of things. We have a very low-drama working environment and we all get along. I couldn't be more blessed with that.
